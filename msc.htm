Structuring a proposal
















 It is useful to structure your proposal in sections. For instance:

[1] Motivation
- Provide the big picture and sell your idea. Why is your topic interesting and
worth pursuing? (ideally not only for nerds)
e.g., Impact on greenhouse gas emissions, local emissions, health effects, energy use, share of GDP.
- You can refer to newspaper articles (The Economist, FAZ, The New York Times, Grist)
to highlight the importance of your topic for business, politics, and society in general.

[2] Research question
- Why is your topic interesting also for nerds?
- What is your contribution?
- What do you want to test or quantify, for instance?
- How does it connect with what exists out there in terms of literature?
- What does it bring to the table?
e.g., does the election of a new mayor have an impact on air pollution of a municipality?
Here, using Google Scholar or other tool might be helful. Recent AI-based tools can be used
but should be cited properly.

[3] Data requirements
- OK, given that you have a hot topic, which data do you need in order to drive your arguments
home?

[4] Research Strategy
- How exactly are you making the case for your argument?
- How exactly are you driving  your arguments home?
- In particular, which regressions do you need to run to make your case, how are you getting
instruments etc?

In summary, you should provide the "What-Why-How" of your thesis in the proposal. Using
bullet points is acceptable as long as they deliver the message.
   
   
Writing-up




 John Cochrane has a useful piece on "how to write a paper" for his PhD students at
 http://faculty.chicagobooth.edu/john.cochrane/research/Papers/#for_phd 
Since you will be writing a thesis instead of a paper, you probably don't want to be as economical
in your writing, but do keep his words in mind. (For instance, if you put a lot of effort in
constructing your dataset, describe it carefully in the "Data" section)
 
   
Where to look for inspiration?


 Google Scholar, SSRN and Journals available from the library, e.g. JSTOR, are good starting points
for your literature search, as are the websites with the conference papers of the big conferences in
Economics, e.g., American Economic Association, Econometric Society (there are several of them
each year), AERE, EAERE, EARIE, International Industrial Organization Conference, European
Economic Association, EARIE etc.
